Activated Carbon Selection Guide for Water Treatment

Activated carbon is a highly porous adsorbent used to remove organic contaminants, taste, odor and color from water. Choosing the right type depends on the raw water quality, treatment goal, contact system and budget.

Raw Material Types

Physical Forms

  • Granular Activated Carbon (GAC): 0.4–2.4 mm particles. Used in fixed-bed filters for continuous flow treatment. Common in municipal drinking water plants.
  • Powdered Activated Carbon (PAC): < 0.15 mm fine powder. Dosed directly into water for intermittent taste/odor events. Lower capital cost.
  • Extruded / Pelletized: Cylindrical shape. Used in vapor phase and some liquid applications.

Key Performance Indicators

  • Iodine number: Measures micropore volume (< 1 nm). Higher = better small-molecule adsorption. Typical: 800–1200 mg/g.
  • Methylene blue value: Measures mesopore volume. Important for color and larger organic molecules. Typical: 100–300 mg/g.
  • Apparent density: Affects contact bed volume and weight-based pricing.
  • Ash content: Indicates purity; lower is better for sensitive applications.
  • Hardness / abrasion number: Important for GAC in backwashed filters.

Application Matching

For drinking water taste and odor removal, coconut shell GAC with high iodine value is generally preferred. For industrial wastewater COD reduction, coal-based GAC or PAC with balanced micro/mesoporosity often offers better value. For color removal from textile or pulp mill effluent, consider wood-based or high-methylene-blue coal-based carbons.

Always verify performance with actual water samples through column tests or batch adsorption tests before full-scale procurement.
