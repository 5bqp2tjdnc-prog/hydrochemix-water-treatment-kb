Water Treatment Chemicals Glossary

A quick reference for common terms used when purchasing water treatment chemicals. Helpful for buyers, procurement teams and plant operators comparing PAC, PAM, activated carbon and related products.

Al2O3 (Alumina)
The active content measure for PAC. Higher Al2O3 means more active ingredient per kilogram. Typical range: 26–30%.
Basicity
For PAC, the ratio of hydroxide to aluminium, expressed as a percentage. Higher basicity usually means better flocculation performance. Typical range: 40–90%.
BOD (Biochemical Oxygen Demand)
A measure of organic pollution in water, representing the oxygen consumed by microorganisms during biodegradation.
COD (Chemical Oxygen Demand)
A measure of all oxidizable organic matter in water. Higher COD means more organic contamination.
Coagulant
A chemical that neutralises the electrical charge of suspended particles, causing them to clump together. PAC and aluminium sulphate are common inorganic coagulants.
Flocculant
A polymer that bridges small coagulated particles into larger, settleable flocs. PAM is the most widely used organic flocculant.
GAC (Granular Activated Carbon)
Activated carbon in granular form (0.4–2.4 mm), typically used in fixed-bed filters for continuous adsorption.
Iodine Number
A standard test that measures the micropore volume and surface area of activated carbon. Higher values indicate better small-molecule adsorption.
Jar Test
A laboratory bench test that simulates coagulation/flocculation to determine optimal chemical type, dosage and pH before full-scale application.
Methylene Blue Value
An indicator of the mesopore volume of activated carbon, relevant for adsorbing larger organic molecules and color bodies.
PAC (Poly Aluminium Chloride)
The most common inorganic coagulant, used for turbidity removal, color reduction and phosphorus precipitation in water and wastewater treatment.
PAC (Powdered Activated Carbon)
Fine-particle activated carbon (< 0.15 mm) dosed directly into water for intermittent taste, odor or organic control. Not to be confused with poly aluminium chloride.
PAM (Polyacrylamide)
A high-molecular-weight organic polymer used as a flocculant, thickener and binder. Available in anionic, cationic and nonionic forms.
Charge Density
The number of charged groups per unit mass of PAM. Influences the polymer's ability to neutralise particle charges.
Molecular Weight
For PAM, a key performance parameter. Higher molecular weight generally produces larger flocs but increases viscosity.
Turbidity
The cloudiness or haziness of a fluid caused by suspended particles. Measured in NTU (Nephelometric Turbidity Units).
Sludge Volume Index (SVI)
A measure of the settling characteristics of activated sludge, expressed as mL/g. Lower SVI means better settling.
COA (Certificate of Analysis)
A supplier-provided document detailing the actual test results of a specific batch of chemical product.
SDS / MSDS
Safety Data Sheet (formerly Material Safety Data Sheet). Contains hazard, handling, storage and first-aid information for a chemical product.
Zeta Potential
The electric potential at the boundary of a suspended particle. Used to optimise coagulant dosage for charge neutralisation.
