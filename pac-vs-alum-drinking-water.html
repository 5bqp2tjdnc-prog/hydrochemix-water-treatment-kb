PAC vs Alum for Drinking Water Treatment

Polyaluminium chloride (PAC) and aluminium sulfate (alum) are both aluminium-based coagulants, but they behave differently in real drinking water plants. The best choice depends on raw water pH, alkalinity, temperature, color, sludge handling capacity and the finished water target.

Working pH and Cold Water Performance

Alum normally performs best in a narrower pH band around neutral conditions. PAC is pre-hydrolyzed and usually works across a wider pH range, so it is often easier to tune on low-alkalinity water, colored surface water and winter operation. In cold water, PAC generally forms stronger floc and settles faster than alum at the same treatment target.

Sludge and Operating Cost

PAC often has a higher unit price than alum, but the operating comparison should include sludge volume, alkalinity correction, filter run time and dose stability. Plants that switch from alum to PAC often evaluate the total cost per cubic meter treated rather than the drum or bag price alone.

When PAC Is Usually Worth Testing First

  • Low-alkalinity raw water where alum depresses pH too much.
  • Cold-season turbidity or color spikes.
  • High-color source water with humic substances.
  • Plants limited by sludge thickening or dewatering capacity.
  • Projects where a stable dose curve is more valuable than the lowest unit price.
